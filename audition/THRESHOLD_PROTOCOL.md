# Threshold Discovery Protocol

*A short guide for running the three-variant threshold experiment consistently, so the instrumentation tells us **what** happened and the facilitator can discover **why**.*

## What we are studying

Presence begins **before** conversation. The threshold is not part of the conversation — it is the moment a stranger decides: *"Yes… I'll tell you something about myself."*

That decision is what we are studying. Everything **before** first entrustment belongs to this experiment. Everything **after** belongs to the Companion, which is unchanged and not under test here. Keep the two separate.

We are not testing three products. We are asking three questions about how trust begins:
- **A — Radical Simplicity:** do people understand how to begin without instruction?
- **B — Presence:** does a wordless breathing presence create confidence before language?
- **C — Invitation:** does a warm welcome lower the emotional threshold?

The primary measure is **First Entrustment Rate** — how many arrivals voluntarily entrust a first meaningful story.

## Inviting testers

- **One variant per person.** Never show a tester more than one — a first impression doesn't come twice, and seeing another variant contaminates it.
- Invite warmly and minimally: *"I'm building something and I'd value ten or fifteen minutes of you simply trying it. There's no task, and no right way to do it."*
- Their **own device, their own space** — phone if that's what they'd naturally pick up. Remote is fine.
- Mention the recording once, plainly, in the invitation: *"It records on-screen interactions to help me learn — no camera, and never the words you write."* The in-app notice restates it; no separate consent click.

## How the facilitator behaves

- **Set them up, then step away.** Presence, not supervision.
- Do not hover, narrate, coach, or react. **Silence is data.**
- If asked "what do I do?", answer only: *"Whatever feels right — there's no wrong way,"* and nothing more.
- **Do not rescue.** If they hesitate, let them hesitate — the hesitation is the finding. Intervene only if something is genuinely broken, and note that it happened.
- Never defend or explain the product.
- Run every session the same way, so the variants stay comparable.

## What must NOT be explained

- Not that it's voice or text, or how to begin.
- Not "you can talk to it" or "type here."
- Not the globe, the Companion, memory, or what LifeBook is.
- Not which variant they have, or that others exist.

The entire point is to see what a person reaches for **unprompted**.

## What to observe (during, without interrupting)

The instrumentation captures the timings; the facilitator captures the texture.
- The **first thing** they do, and how long until they do it (voice / text / globe / nothing).
- **Hesitation** — where it happens, how long, what they seemed to be looking for.
- Whether they **spoke or typed**, and whether they switched.
- If in person: expression and sound — a smile, a frown, a sigh (observed, never filmed).
- Whether they **crossed the threshold** (entrusted a first real thing), and roughly when.
- Whether they **continued** past the first exchange.

## Questions to ask afterward (only afterward)

Open and non-leading, in their own words:
- "What did you think this was?"
- "What was going through your mind when it first appeared?"
- "Was there a moment you weren't sure what to do?"
- "Did it feel like using an app, or something else?"
- "What made you decide to say something — or to hold back?"
- "How did it feel?" — then be quiet and let them fill the silence.

**Avoid:** "Did you like it?", "Was it easy?", and any yes/no or satisfaction question. They invite politeness, not truth.

## The line we keep

If we ever find ourselves polishing rather than learning, stop and ask whether the effort answers the question. Three questions, not three products. Once the variants are deployed, we stop designing and start observing.

Reality gets the next vote.
